## Overview
This project analyses how **skill and education mismatch** affect labour force participation, using **hours worked and wages** as key indicators.

The goal is to translate an academic econometrics study into **applied, policy-relevant insights**.

---

## Why This Matters
Skill mismatch is a key driver of:
- Labour market inefficiency  
- Wage disparities  
- Underutilisation of human capital  

Understanding this is critical for:
- Workforce planning  
- Education policy  
- Economic productivity  

---

## Methodology
- **Dataset:** Longitudinal labour panel (2019–2022)  
- **Sample:** ~5,300 working individuals  
- **Approach:**
  - Pooled OLS  
  - Fixed Effects  
  - **Random Effects (primary model)**  

The use of panel methods helps control for **unobserved individual differences**, improving reliability of results.

---

## Key Findings

- **Over-educated workers**
  - Earn ~6–13% less  
  - Work fewer hours  

- **Over-skilled workers**
  - Earn ~5–6% less  
  - No significant gain in participation  

- **Under-educated workers**
  - Work more hours  
  - Slightly higher earnings (in some models)  

---

## Core Insight
Skill mismatch leads to **inefficient allocation of labour**, reducing productivity and economic output.

---

## Implications

**Policy**
- Improve education-to-job alignment  
- Invest in reskilling and workforce planning  

**Business**
- Address underutilisation of skilled employees  
- Improve job matching and role design
